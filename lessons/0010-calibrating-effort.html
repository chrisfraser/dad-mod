The Lifts · L7

Calibrating Effort

"Stop 1–3 reps short of failure" only works if you can feel where failure is — and as a beginner, you almost certainly think it's closer than it is.

⏱ ~12 minutes• 🎚️ With a rep-by-rep simulator• The skill that makes volume work
◎ Why this lesson

Your whole program runs on RIR — "leave 1–3 reps in the tank." But that instruction is useless if your internal "I'm done" alarm is wrong. The evidence says a beginner's alarm goes off way too early. This lesson recalibrates it, so your hard sets are genuinely hard and your volume actually counts.

1RIR and RPE — one simple scale

RIR (Reps In Reserve) is how many more reps you could have done. RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) is just the flip side on a 1–10 scale.1 Your "RIR 1–3" is simply RPE 7–9:

The lifting RPE ↔ RIR scale (Zourdos / Helms)
RPEReps in reserveWhat it feels like
100Failure — last rep was an all-out grind
91Definitely one left, no more
82Two left; bar is clearly slowing
73Three left; bar still moving well
5–64–6Effort building, but reps still smooth

2The beginner trap: you stop too early

Here's the finding that matters most. When untrained people are asked to predict how many reps they have left, they massively underestimate — they feel "almost done" while several clean reps remain. In one study of 141 people, less-experienced lifters underpredicted failure by ~4–5 reps; even experienced lifters were off by ~1–2.23

⚠ What this means for you

When you think "that's RPE 10, I'm done," you may actually be at RPE 5–6 with 4–5 reps left. If you stop there, your "hard" sets aren't hard, and your training is quietly under-dosed. The fix isn't to grind everything to failure — it's to learn what the top of the scale really feels like.

3Read the bar, not the feeling

The most reliable signal of approaching failure is involuntary bar slowdown: as you near failure the lifting phase gets slower even though you're pushing as hard as you can. Fast bar = reps in the bank.4 Play a set — lift reps, watch the speed, and rack when you'd stop. Then see how many you really had left.

🎚️ Last-rep cue simulator — when would YOU rack it?
Reps you think you had left:
Lift reps one at a time and watch the bar speed. Rack the set when you'd normally stop — then guess your reps in reserve.

4The cues that tell you you're close

  • Bar slows on the way up. The last 1–2 reps "grind" — the objective gold-standard cue.
  • Form starts to drift. Range shortens, the path wobbles, hips shoot up on a squat.
  • The "$100 test." At the point you want to stop, ask: "If someone paid me $100, could I get 2 more clean reps?" If "easily" — you're at RIR 3+. If "maybe one, ugly" — you're at RIR ~1.

6Your calibration routine

✓ Recalibrate over a few weeks

Pick one safe machine (leg press, machine chest press). (1) Once, take a set to true failure so you learn what 0 RIR feels like. (2) Each session after, predict the rep where you'll hit RIR 2 — stop there — then do one more set and count how many you actually had left. (3) Most beginners find they were stopping 2–4 reps early; over a few weeks the gap shrinks. (4) Re-test to failure about monthly as you get stronger.4

What you can now say

  1. RIR 1–3 = RPE 7–9 — leave one to three reps in the tank.
  2. Beginners stop too early, underestimating reps left by ~4–5.
  3. The reliable cue is the bar slowing involuntarily, plus form drift and the "$100 test".
  4. Hypertrophy RIR 1–2; heavy compounds RIR 2–4; true failure rarely.
  5. Calibrate: meet failure once on a machine, then predict-and-check for a few weeks.
