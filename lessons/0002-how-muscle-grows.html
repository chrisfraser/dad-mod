How Muscle Actually Grows

What's happening inside the muscle between your sets — and why the first weeks of "gains" are mostly a lie the tape measure tells.

⏱ ~12 minutes• 🔬 Mechanism, with one myth busted• Why the levers work
◎ Why this lesson

Lesson 1 told you what to do — volume, near-failure effort, protein. This one shows you why those work, by following a single hard set down to the molecule. Get this and the plan stops being a list of rules and starts being something you understand. It also sets honest expectations for what your mirror will show in month one.

1The signal is mechanical tension

A muscle doesn't grow because it's "tired" or "pumped". It grows because its fibres were put under high mechanical tension — a hard pull — and that physical force is converted into a chemical growth signal, a process called mechanotransduction. Strain on the fibre's internal scaffolding (the filamin-C / BAG3 complex is the leading candidate1) switches on mTORC1, the master control for building new muscle protein.

That last arrow matters: tension flips the switch, but you only build new tissue if the raw material — protein — is there. Tension without protein is a builder with no bricks.

2The famous "three mechanisms", corrected

You'll read everywhere that muscle grows from three things: mechanical tension, metabolic stress ("the burn"), and muscle damage ("the soreness"). That 2010 framework5 is a useful map, but the science has since moved on1:

Mechanical tension
Primary driver

The real signal. Heavy, hard reps create it. This is the one you train for.

Metabolic stress
Minor / uncertain

"The burn." Probably matters mostly because high-rep sets drive fibres toward failure — not magic on its own.

Muscle damage
Not a driver

Soreness is a side-effect, not a goal. It can even divert resources to repair. Chasing soreness is a mistake.

⌗ So: don't chase soreness or the burn

If a session leaves you wrecked and sore, that's damage, not a better growth signal. Aim for hard, controlled tension across your weekly sets — and recover. Feeling fine the next day does not mean the session "didn't work".

3Synthesis up, breakdown down — net is what counts

After a hard session, muscle protein synthesis (MPS) rises and stays elevated for roughly 24–48 hours.2 But training also raises muscle protein breakdown. Growth is the net balance:

growth = (protein synthesised) − (protein broken down)

Fasted, that balance stays slightly negative even after training. Eating protein flips it positive — which is the whole reason behind the ~1.6–2.2 g/kg/day target, and why spreading it across the day keeps you topped up through that 24–48 h window.4 This is the molecular cash-out of Lesson 1's protein rule.

4Why the scale lies in month one

Here's the result that re-calibrates every beginner. When untrained men started lifting, their measured muscle size jumped early — but biopsies showed that early jump was mostly swelling and repair, not new contractile muscle. True fibre growth only became the main story after about three to ten weeks, once the early damage settled down.3 Drag through the weeks:

★ The expectation this sets

Month one will feel amazing — fast strength, a fuller look, the scale moving. Enjoy it, but know that the visible size is largely fluid. The real muscle is being laid down underneath and shows up over months. This is exactly why the honest year-one goal is ~5–9 kg, not the overnight transformation the internet sells.

5Why "1–3 reps short" still hits the big fibres

Your nervous system recruits motor units (a nerve plus the fibres it controls) in a fixed order — small and weak first, large and powerful last. This is Henneman's size principle.1 The big, high-threshold fibres — the ones with the most growth potential — only switch on when the effort gets genuinely hard:

Easy early reps — small fibres only
Mid set — medium fibres join in
Last hard reps (≈1–3 from failure) — big fibres finally recruited

So you reach the muscle-building fibres in the final few reps before failure — which is exactly why stopping at RIR 1–3 works, and why grinding all the way to failure adds lots of fatigue for barely any extra recruitment. Heavy loads get you there immediately; lighter loads get you there if you take the set close to failure.

6Two footnotes worth knowing

Myonuclei & "muscle memory." Muscle fibres are built from many nuclei, and big growth needs more of them, donated by resident stem cells (satellite cells). In animals these extra nuclei seem to stick around during time off, a proposed basis for "muscle memory" — but whether that holds in humans is not settled.6 Either way, re-building lost muscle is faster than the first time.

Myofibrillar vs. sarcoplasmic. The growth that lasts and adds force is myofibrillar (more actin/myosin). A separate "sarcoplasmic" growth (more fluid and glycogen) is real but debated and probably minor for you.7 Train for tension; you'll get the kind that counts.

What you can now say

  1. Growth's real signal is mechanical tension, sensed and converted to a build order (mTORC1 → synthesis).
  2. Soreness and "the burn" aren't the goal — tension is; damage is just a side-effect.
  3. Training lifts synthesis for 24–48 h; protein turns the net balance positive.
  4. Early measured size is mostly swelling; real muscle accrues over weeks to months.
  5. The last few reps before failure recruit the big fibres — why RIR 1–3 works.
