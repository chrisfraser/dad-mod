# Weight Training Glossary

The canonical vocabulary for this workspace. Lessons and the program card use these exact
terms. Seeded with the essentials a beginner meets in week one; promoted/revised as understanding deepens.

## Terms

**Rep (repetition)**:
One complete execution of an exercise — the lift and the return.
_Avoid_: count, movement

**Set**:
A group of consecutive reps with no rest. In this workspace "set" always means a *working set*; warm-up sets are tracked separately.
_Avoid_: round

**Hypertrophy**:
Muscle growth, driven mainly by mechanical tension accumulated through hard sets over time.
_Avoid_: bulking, getting big, toning

**Recomposition (recomp)**:
Gaining muscle and losing fat at the same time. Most achievable for the untrained and the overweight — exactly this learner's profile.
_Avoid_: toning

**Volume**:
The total amount of hard work for a muscle, counted as hard sets per muscle per week. The strongest lever for hypertrophy.
_Avoid_: how much you do

**Counting hard sets (how to)**:
How you measure weekly [[#volume]]. Count only hard working sets (~1–3 [[#rir]]) — not warm-ups. Add them up across the whole week and all sessions, not per workout. A compound counts for each major muscle it trains (a row = 1 back set; a bench = 1 chest set). Count primary movers fully; don't fuss over indirect arm work.

**Frequency**:
How many times per week a muscle is trained. Mainly a way to *distribute* weekly volume into recoverable sessions.

**Compound lift**:
A multi-joint exercise training several muscles at once (squat, row, press). The backbone of a time-efficient programme.
_Avoid_: big lift

**Isolation exercise**:
A single-joint exercise targeting one muscle (e.g. biceps curl). Accessory work, not the backbone.

**1RM (one-rep max)**:
The most weight you can lift for a single rep. Loads are often described as a percentage of 1RM.

**RIR (Reps In Reserve)**:
How many more reps you could have done before failure. "Stop at 2 RIR" = stop with 2 reps left in the tank. The practical effort gauge in this workspace.
_Avoid_: how hard it felt

**RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion)**:
A 1–10 effort rating where 10 = failure. RPE 8 ≈ 2 RIR. The inverse view of RIR.
_Avoid_: effort score

**Progressive overload**:
Systematically increasing the demand on a muscle over time — usually by adding load or reps. The engine of all progress.
_Avoid_: pushing harder, levelling up

**Double progression**:
The progression rule used here: work within a rep range at a fixed load until you hit the top of the range on all sets, then add the smallest load and rebuild. Self-regulating; outlasts session-by-session linear progression.

**Linear progression**:
Adding a fixed weight every session (e.g. +2.5 kg). Works briefly for novices, then stalls; we use double progression instead.

**Full-body split**:
A session structure where every workout trains the whole body. Suits 3 days/week — each muscle gets trained ~3× without long sessions.

**Hinge**:
The hip-dominant movement pattern (deadlift, Romanian deadlift, hip thrust). Trains the posterior chain — glutes, hamstrings, back.

**Neutral grip**:
Palms-facing-each-other / hammer grip that keeps the wrist straight (no extension). The default grip for this learner given the scaphoid history.
_Avoid_: hammer grip (use as alias only)

**Anabolic resistance**:
The blunted muscle-building response to training/protein seen mainly in older age (65+). Largely overcome by the training stimulus itself; only a minor factor at 40.

## Mechanism (how growth happens)

**Mechanotransduction**:
How a muscle fibre converts mechanical force (tension) into the chemical signals that switch on growth (mTORC1 → protein synthesis). The reason mechanical tension, not soreness, is the real stimulus.

**Muscle protein synthesis (MPS)**:
The building of new muscle protein. A hard session raises MPS for ~24–48 h; net growth = MPS minus breakdown, which dietary protein tips positive.
_Avoid_: "the pump builds muscle"

**Motor-unit recruitment / size principle**:
Henneman's rule that motor units are recruited small-to-large as effort rises. The big, high-growth fibres only switch on near the end of a hard set — why [[#rir]] 1–3 (or heavy load) matters.

## Mechanics (of a rep)

**Moment arm (lever arm)**:
The perpendicular distance from a force's line of action to the joint. Torque = force × moment arm; a lift is hardest where the weight's moment arm is longest.

**Strength curve**:
How a lift's difficulty changes through its range, set by the moment arm: ascending (squat/press), descending (rows/pulldowns), or bell-shaped (curls).

**Range of motion (ROM)**:
How far a joint travels in a rep. Full ROM grows at least as much as partial; loading the muscle at long (stretched) lengths is especially valuable.

**Bracing (360° / Valsalva)**:
Stiffening the trunk to protect the spine under load: breathe into the belly so it expands all the way around, brace the abs as if to take a punch, hold through the hard part of the rep. Used on every working rep of the big lifts.

**Scapular set / retraction**:
Pulling the shoulder blades down and back ("into your back pockets") and holding them before pressing or pulling. A stable base on presses; engages and protects the shoulder on pulls.

## Metabolic health

**GLUT4**:
The transporter that opens a door to let glucose into a muscle cell. Moved to the cell surface by two separate signals — insulin, and (independently) muscle contraction.

**Insulin sensitivity**:
How readily insulin moves glucose from blood into tissue (mostly muscle). Higher is better; resistance training raises it acutely (~24–72 h) and chronically. Low sensitivity = insulin resistance, the earliest step toward type 2 diabetes.

**Myokine**:
A signalling molecule released by contracting muscle (e.g. IL-6) acting on fat, liver and brain — making muscle an endocrine organ. The transient exercise IL-6 spike is beneficial, unlike chronic IL-6 from fat.

## Fuel, recovery & supplements

**Energy balance**:
Calories eaten minus calories burned. A deficit drives fat loss, a surplus drives gain. A small deficit (~300–500 kcal) + high protein + lifting drives [[#recomposition]].

**Recovery / stimulus–recovery–adaptation (SRA)**:
Training is only a stimulus; muscle is built during the recovery after it (synthesis elevated ~24–48 h). Why rest days and sleep are part of the programme, not time off it.

**Leucine**:
The amino acid that triggers [[#muscle-protein-synthesis]]. A meal needs ~2.5–3 g leucine (≈20–40 g quality protein) to maximally switch on growth — the reason for ~0.4 g/kg protein per meal.

**Creatine / phosphocreatine**:
A compound stored in muscle that rapidly recharges ATP for short, hard efforts, letting you train slightly harder. Taken as 3–5 g/day of monohydrate; the most evidence-backed supplement for muscle and strength.
_Avoid_: "creatine is a steroid"
